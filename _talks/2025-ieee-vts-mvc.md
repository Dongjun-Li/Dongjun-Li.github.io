Presented our award-winning solution for the IEEE VTS Motor Vehicles Challenge 2025 at the IEEE VPPC 2025 special session. Our team, Energy Storage Lab, won the 1st Place Award in the challenge on energy management and control of a marine electric propulsion system.

I led a three-member team from the National University of Singapore, together with Haoxuan Dong and Liangcai Xu, under the mentorship of Prof. Ziyou Song. The challenge focused on the energy management of an electric tugboat propulsion system powered by a battery pack and a supercapacitor module. The objective was to design a real-time energy management strategy that could improve system efficiency and component lifetime without using any prior knowledge of the future operating profile.

A key difficulty was that conventional planning-based optimization methods were not directly applicable, because the future speed profile and operating mode of the vessel were unknown during evaluation. In addition, the available control input was indirect: instead of directly assigning the power split between the battery and supercapacitor, the controller had to regulate the torque imbalance between the two winding sets of a dual three-phase permanent magnet motor.

To address these challenges, we developed an adaptive real-time energy management strategy based on high-level control rules and a derived mapping between torque imbalance and energy-source power distribution. This design allowed the controller to respond effectively to uncertain operating conditions while reducing undesirable behaviors such as excessive energy consumption, rapid battery current fluctuations, and inefficient motor winding utilization.

This experience strengthened my ability to lead a technical team under time constraints, translate complex system-level requirements into implementable control logic, and communicate engineering solutions in a competitive international setting.
